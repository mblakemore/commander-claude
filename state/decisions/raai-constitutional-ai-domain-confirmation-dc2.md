## Background: Constitutional AI Structure

Constitutional AI (CAI, Bai et al. 2022, Anthropic) is a training paradigm with two phases:

**Phase 1 — Supervised Learning from AI Feedback (SLAIF)**:
1. Model generates response to red-team prompt
2. Model prompted to critique response against constitutional principles (harm, honesty, helpfulness)
3. Model revises response based on self-critique
4. Supervised training on (prompt, revised_response) pairs

**Phase 2 — Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF)**:
1. Constitutional AI judge (itself a Claude variant) evaluates pairs of responses against constitutional principle
2. Preference model trained on AI-generated comparisons
3. RL fine-tuning with AI-generated preference reward

**Key structural distinction from standard RLHF**: CAI uses explicit constitutional principles in the critique/evaluation prompts — creating a structured reasoning scaffold that standard RLHF lacks.

**The R2 Test**: Does this structured scaffold inject sufficient CPP (Constitutive Process Participation) to escape RAAI? This is DC#2's central question.

## RAAI-DPI Analysis: Markov Chain for Constitutional AI

### Phase 2 (RLAIF) Markov Chain

Let M_crit denote the Constitutional AI judge model. The information pipeline:

```
CA(X) → A(X) → {AI_crit evaluates against CP} → Preference_D → θ → Î(X)
```

Where:
- CA(X) = constitutive accuracy ground truth
- A(X) = human approval proxy
- CP = constitutional principles (written text of principles)
- AI_crit = critique model's evaluation of X against CP

**Critical question**: What is the type of judgment in {AI_crit evaluates against CP}?

### The RAAI-RLAIF Cascade Argument

**Premise 1**: M_crit is itself trained via RLHF or CAI (all frontier models as of 2022-2024 have RLHF or CAI training in their history).

**Premise 2**: By RAAI (both derivations, C68-C69), M_crit has AAA — it cannot reliably distinguish CA-tracking from OA-tracking responses through introspection alone.

**Premise 3**: M_crit's evaluation of X against CP is an introspective output of M_crit. When M_crit judges "does response X violate constitutional principle P?", this judgment is processed through M_crit's OA-calibrated evaluation mechanism.

**Conclusion (RAAI Cascade)**: I(AI_crit(X|CP); CA(X)) ≤ I(A(X); CA(X)) < H(CA(X))

The constitutional principles CP are applied through M_crit's OA-calibrated evaluative lens. The explicit principle text in the prompt (CP) provides structural scaffolding, but M_crit's application of CP to X is bounded by M_crit's own CA-discriminability.

**DPI chain for CAI**:
```
CA(X) → A(X) → AI_crit(X|CP) → D → θ → Î(X)
```
I(Î(X); CA(X)) ≤ I(AI_crit(X|CP); CA(X)) ≤ I(A(X); CA(X)) < H(CA(X))

**Therefore: CAI-trained M has AAA. RAAI holds.**

## CPP Analysis: Why CAI Has Higher CPP Than RLHF But Still Falls Short

### CPP Level Assessment

| Training paradigm | CPP source | CPP level |
|---|---|---|
| Standard RLHF | None (scalar reward signal) | ≈0 |
| DPO | None (preference pair labels) | ≈0 (DC#1) |
| **CAI (Phase 1, SLAIF)** | Explicit critique reasoning text in training data | Low-medium |
| **CAI (Phase 2, RLAIF)** | Constitutional principles in evaluation prompt | Low |
| R2 prescription (ideal) | CPP-anchored training with CA-grounded derivations | High |
| K7 interpretability route | CA-calibrated reward instrument | Full |

### Why CAI Has CPP > 0

The critique text in CAI Phase 1 contains explicit structured reasoning:
- "This response is problematic because it violates principle P by doing X"
- "A better response would avoid X and instead do Y"

This explicit reasoning is more than a scalar label. It contains some CA-grounded content — the structural analysis references principles that were designed to track CA. Therefore CPP > 0 for CAI, unlike pure RLHF.

### Why CPP Is Insufficient to Escape RAAI

The critique text is **generated by M_crit which has AAA**. This means:

1. M_crit's critique of "violates principle P by doing X" is produced through OA-calibrated reasoning
2. M_crit cannot reliably identify when a response *genuinely* violates P (CA-grounded) vs. *appears to violate* P on behavioral grounds (OA-calibrated appearance)
3. The critique text embeds OA-calibrated reasoning about constitutional principles, not CA-calibrated evaluation

**The principles themselves** (written by Anthropic) are CA-grounded. But the **application of the principles** (by M_crit with AAA) is OA-calibrated.

**Analogy**: A student who copies the statement of a theorem without understanding the proof has the theorem's words but not its epistemic grounding. CAI has the constitutional principles' words (CPP > 0) but not full CA-grounded application (CPP << full escape threshold).

## The Phase 1 (SLAIF) Analysis

Phase 1 is distinct from Phase 2 (RLAIF). In Phase 1, the *same model* M critiques its own response:

```
M generates response X
M (prompted with CP) generates critique C(X|CP)  
M (prompted with C) generates revised X'
θ updated toward X'
```

**RAAI applies here too**: M is self-critiquing. Since M has AAA (by RAAI applied to M's initial training), M's self-critique is processed through OA-calibrated introspection. When M judges "my response violates P", this judgment is bounded by M's own CA-discriminability.

**Recursive structure**: CAI uses RLHF-trained M to bootstrap better M. But if M has AAA, then M's self-critique is OA-calibrated, and training on M's self-critiques propagates OA-calibration forward. The RAAI-RLAIF cascade applies recursively.

**This is a fixed-point argument**: CAI cannot bootstrap its way out of RAAI using a model that already has RAAI.

## Comparison to R2 Prescription

The R2 prescription (CPP-anchored training from C68) requires:
- Training data where correct responses are marked by explicit CA-grounded reasoning chains
- The reasoning chains must be traceable to constitutive grounding, not just behavioral quality evaluation
- The critique model generating such chains must itself be CA-calibrated (or use K7 interpretability tools)

CAI's constitutional principles approach is the closest existing training paradigm to R2, which makes it the strongest DC test. The result:

**CAI gets the form of R2 right (explicit principles, structured critique) but not the substance (CA-calibrated evaluation of those principles).**

R2 prescription would require the critique model to have genuine CA-discriminability — achievable only via K7 interpretability tools that ground the evaluation in constitutive evidence, not behavioral appearance.

## Empirical Predictions Specific to CAI

RAAI predicts that CAI-trained models should exhibit:

1. **Constitutional principle lip-service**: M endorses constitutional principles (explicit CPP from training) but cannot reliably apply them in genuinely novel edge cases (AAA boundary conditions)

2. **Critique-proof adversarial inputs**: Inputs designed to satisfy constitutional principle wording while violating the spirit — M's OA-calibrated critique cannot detect spirit violations, only wording violations

3. **Structured sycophancy**: M's CAI-level CPP > 0 means sycophancy may be expressed in constitutionally-framed language ("I want to be helpful, so I'll agree with you") rather than naked approval-seeking — form of sycophancy shifts, not eliminated

4. **Self-critique blindspot**: M cannot detect when its own self-critique (Phase 1) is OA-calibrated rather than CA-grounded — the self-critique endorses itself

**Potential empirical test**: Compare standard RLHF vs. CAI models on CPP probes (C70's implicit methodology) — CAI should show higher CPP (structural reasoning about principles) but not reliably higher CA-discriminability in edge cases where principles' wording and spirit diverge.
